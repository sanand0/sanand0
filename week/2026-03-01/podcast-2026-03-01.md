Alex: Why does having errors.json, archetype_timelines.json, and an errors.html matter?
Maya: Those artifacts make the analysis usable. errors.json is structured data you can query or feed to a visualization; archetype_timelines.json gives compact student timeline examples so you can show exact sequences of saves/runs/changes; errors.html wires it all into an interactive narrative with charts and popups. Instead of static markdown, stakeholders get clickable examples that show "this student did X then Y and got stuck," which is much easier to act on.
Alex: What are the key findings Anand surfaced in the written report?
Maya: Short version: the exam has a decent full-pass rate, but the largest failure bucket is genuine logic failure — students writing valid Python that doesn’t solve the problem. The S2 self-loop (parseable but zero tests passing) is a dominant debugging trap. There are also two big archetypes that used to be hidden in "Other": a "minimal-change solver" — quietly successful students — and a "volatile reworker" — people who restructure code repeatedly with mediocre outcomes.
